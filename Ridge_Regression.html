Ridge Regression; GCV

Jie Peng| STA 206 Fall 2021

Ridge regression is a commonly used method to deal with severe multicollinearity (including perfect collinearity). Recall that, multicollinearity results in inflated variances of the fitted regression coefficients and poor interpretability and generalizability of the fitted regression model. One idea is to constrain the fitted regression coefficients to achieve variance reduction, at the expense of introducing some biases. With suitably chosen tuning parameter, ridge regression can achieve good bias-variance trade-off.

Ridge estimator

In the following, assume the multiple regression model: \[ Y=X\beta+\epsilon, ~~ E(\epsilon)=0, ~\sigma^2\{\epsilon\}=\sigma^2 \mathbf{I}. \]

The ridge estimator is the minimizer of the \(\ell_2\) penalized least-squares criterion: \[ Q_\lambda(b)=(Y-Xb)^T (Y-Xb)+\lambda b^T b, ~~~ b \in \mathbb{R}^p, \] where \(\lambda \geq 0\) is the tuning parameter and the term \(\lambda b^T b=\lambda ||b||^2_2\) may be viewed as a ``penalty" on the size of the regression coefficients. When \(\lambda=0\), there is no penalty and the above is simply the least-squares criterion.

Ridge regression is usually applied to standardized X variables (i.e., centered and scaled) and centered response variable. The purpose is to (i) make the amount of penalty comparable across different regression coefficients (ii) make the regression intercept unaffected by the penalty as we usually do not want to penalize the intercept.

Setting the gradient of \(Q_\lambda(\cdot)\) with respect to \(b\) to zero gives the normal equation: \[ \frac{\partial Q_\lambda(b)}{\partial b}=2X^TX b+2 \lambda b -2 X^T Y=0 \] The solution of the normal equation is the ridge estimator: \[ \hat{\beta}_\lambda=(X^T X + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} X^T Y \] The ordinary least-squares estimator (OLS) \(\hat{\beta}_{ols}\) corresponds to \(\lambda=0\). On the other hand, when \(\lambda>0\), the ridge estimator is shrunk towards zero: \(||\hat{\beta}_\lambda||_2 < ||\hat{\beta}_{ols}||_2\). Note that, if both \(X\) and \(Y\) are centered, the estimated intercept \(\hat{\beta}_{0,\lambda}\) will always be zero (and not dependent on \(\lambda\)).

The ridge estimator is a biased estimator: \[ E(\hat{\beta}_\lambda)= (X^T X + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} X^T X \beta, \] and the amount of bias increases with the increase of \(\lambda\).

On the other hand, the variance of the ridge estimator is \[ \sigma^2\{\hat{\beta}_\lambda\}=\sigma^2 (X^T X + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} X^T X (X^T X + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \]

and the variance decreases with the increase of \(\lambda\).

Indeed, there exists a \(\lambda \geq 0\) such that the (overall) mean squared estimation error (msee) of the regression coefficients

\[\sum_{k=1}^{p-1} msee(\hat{\beta}_{k,\lambda})=\sum_{k=1}^{p-1} var(\hat{\beta}_{k,\lambda})+ bias^2(\hat{\beta}_{k,\lambda}), ~~where ~~ bias(\hat{\beta}_{k,\lambda}):=E(\hat{\beta}_{k,\lambda})-\beta_k\]

is the smallest. Therefore, in theory, ridge regression will always beat the ordinary least-squares regression. However, in practice, we do not know the \(\lambda\) that achieves the optimal bias-variance trade-off. Instead, we need to resort to tuning parameter selection. One approach is based on the generalized cross validation (GCV ) criterion discussed below.

Generalized cross validation (GCV)

Ridge regression conducts a linear smoothing of \(Y\):
\[ \hat{Y}_\lambda=X \hat{\beta}_\lambda = X (X^T X + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} X^T Y = S_\lambda Y, \] where \(S_\lambda=X (X^T X + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} X^T\) is referred to as a smoothing operator/matrix and the trace of \(S_\lambda\) is referred to as the effective number of parameters.

Similarly as in ordinary least squares, the deleted residuals can be expressed through the corresponding (ordinary) residuals: \[ Y_i-\hat{Y}_{i(i), \lambda}=\frac{Y_i-\hat{Y}_{i,\lambda}}{1-S_{ii,\lambda}}, ~~~i=1,\cdots, n, \] where \(S_{ii, \lambda}\) is the \(i\)th diagonal element of the smoothing matrix \(S_{\lambda}\).

Notes:

  • The above can be derived in the same way as for OLS.
  • The above holds for any linear smoothing of the form: \(\hat{Y}=S Y\) that arises from a least squares projection.

The leave-one-out-cross-validation criterion is defined as: \[ CV(\lambda)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i-\hat{Y}_{i(i), \lambda})^2=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (\frac{Y_i-\hat{Y}_{i,\lambda}}{1-S_{ii,\lambda}})^2. \]

The tuning parameter \(\lambda\) can then be chosen to minimize \(CV(\lambda)\).

Under some settings, \(trace(S_\lambda)\) is easier to compute than individual diagonals \(S_{ii,\lambda}\), this leads to the generalized cross validation (GCV) approximation: \[ GCV(\lambda)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (\frac{Y_i-\hat{Y}_{i,\lambda}}{1-trace(S_\lambda)/n})^2, \] where individual \(S_{ii,\lambda}\) is replaced by the average \(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n S_{ii,\lambda}=trace(S_\lambda)/n\). In addition to the ease of computation, another advantage of GCV is that it alleviates (to some degree) the tendency of leave-one-out-CV criterion favoring too small \(\lambda\) which leads to under-smoothing and overfitting.

We can use the lm.ridge() function in the MASS library to fit ridge regression on a sequence of \(\lambda\). Since the estimated ridge coefficients tend to change a lot as \(\lambda\) changes in the vicinity of zero, the \(\lambda\) sequence can be chosen to be equally spaced on the logarithm scale so \(\lambda\) is more densely spaced near zero:

library(MASS)
lambda.s=exp(seq(log(1e-3), log(3), length.out = 1000)) ## sequence of lambda: equally spaced on log-scale
par(mfrow=c(1,2))
plot(lambda.s, ylab=expression(lambda))
plot(lambda.s, ylab=expression(lambda), log="y")  

par(mfrow=c(1,1))
fit.ridge=lm.ridge(Y~., data=bodyfat,lambda=lambda.s)
summary(fit.ridge)

Performance evaluation: ridge vs.OLS

In practice, due to lack of knowledge of the ground truth, it is often hard to decide how well a method works or whether one method works better than another. For this purpose, we can partition the data into a training set used for model building (including model selection or tuning parameter selection) and a testing set used solely for performance evaluation.

Lets look at the meatspec data from the faraway library. This data has fat content along with 100 channel spectrum of absorbances measured on \(215\) meat samples. The goal is to build a model using the absorbances measurements (easy to obtain) to predict the fat content (hard to measure through analytical chemistry).

This example demonstrates that compared with OLS, ridge regression (with suitably chosen \(\lambda\)) is less susceptible to overfitting and generalizes better to new observations due to reduced variance. We can also repeat the random split a number of times so that the performances evaluation is not based on a single split:

rmse.ols<-matrix(0, B,2)
rmse.ridge<-matrix(0, B,2)
